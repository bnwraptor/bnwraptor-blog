The Country That Turns Off the Web for Football

March 23, 2026

Spain is a first-world country. High-speed trains, universal healthcare, reliable electricity. But every weekend, when LaLiga matches kick off, something strange happens: large chunks of the internet just stop working in Spain.

I'm not exaggerating. When Barcelona plays Real Madrid, thousands of websites become inaccessible across the entire country. A Spanish startup called Tinybird watched their infrastructure vanish for hours. Game servers stopped responding. Developers posted panicked messages on Reddit: "My game's server is blocked in Spain whenever there's a football match on." Someone else complained they could not access 60% of the internet during matchdays. This goes beyond inconvenience. It affects real businesses, real services, real people trying to get things done.

This is not a natural outage. It is deliberate. And it happens almost every weekend during football season, which in Spain runs from August to May. The blocking has become so routine that it is now just part of life in one of Europe largest economies.

The Culprit

The culprit is LaLiga and its aggressive war on piracy. LaLiga convinced Spanish courts to let ISPs block IP addresses linked to unauthorized streams. Sounds reasonable. The problem is the method. They do not block specific domains or content. They block entire IP ranges, wholesale. Since Cloudflare hosts millions of websites on shared infrastructure, when LaLiga blacklists a Cloudflare IP, every site on that IP goes dark. Vercel, GitHub Pages, BunnyCDN: all caught in the crossfire, with no warning, no recourse, no way to plead their case.

In December 2024, Commercial Court No. 6 of Barcelona authorized this approach. Cloudflare immediately sued, calling the blocks "disproportionate" and "indiscriminate." The companys CEO, Matthew Prince, put it bluntly: this was "the digital equivalent of shutting down electricity to an entire city because one house is stealing." A Spanish court disagreed. The blocking continued.

The technical reason this happens is equally frustrating. Normally, ISPs can block specific domains by inspecting the Server Name Indication (SNI) header during the TLS handshake. But Cloudflare deployed a privacy feature called Encrypted Client Hello (ECH), which hides that hostname information. LaLiga's response was to throw up their hands and block the whole IP instead of investing in more precise tools. It is a sledgehammer where a scalpel should have been used.

The Escalation

By February 2026, LaLiga had escalated further. A Barcelona court granted them precautionary measures against NordVPN and ProtonVPN, ordering those services to block 16 alleged pirate sites. The key detail: the court allowed dynamic blocking, meaning LaLiga can update the list of banned IP addresses in real-time as they identify new targets. NordVPN and ProtonVPN both said they had not been officially notified of the orders. The blocking machinery keeps grinding forward regardless.

There is something almost comedic about the collateral damage. At various points, the blocks have taken down Vercels infrastructure, multiple Steam game servers, Redsys payment processing, regional media outlets, and even the United States governments Freedom.gov portal. Yes, the US governments own anti-censorship website got caught in LaLigas net. Nobody at LaLiga seems to be quality-checking these IP lists before they go live.

Here is what really gets me about this story: it barely works. Pirates always find a way. New IPs, mirror sites, alternative CDNs: the streaming ecosystem adapts faster than LaLiga can block it. Within days of each new block, streams are back up on fresh infrastructure. All this disruption to legitimate businesses and startup infrastructure, and the people LaLiga is actually trying to stop barely notice.

A Global Pattern

Italy has a similar system called Piracy Shield. It lets rightsholders trigger DNS and IP blocks within 30 minutes, no judicial oversight required. France, India, Russia: country after country is experimenting with infrastructure-level blocking as a shortcut for content enforcement. Spain is just the most aggressive example.

LaLiga has every right to protect its content. Football broadcasting rights are enormously valuable, and piracy genuinely hurts clubs, players, and the entire sports ecosystem. But there is something deeply alarming about a country that routinely takes down thousands of legitimate services, government portals, startup infrastructure, game servers, payment systems, just to make it slightly harder to watch football for free.

Spain wants to be seen as a modern European economy. And yet, every weekend, the internet there behaves like something from a developing nation with authoritarian tendencies. That is the real story here. This is not just about piracy or copyright. It is about what happens when private interests are given legal tools to break public infrastructure, and nobody in a position of authority says stop.

That is Spain. A developed country that turns off the web whenever the match starts.
